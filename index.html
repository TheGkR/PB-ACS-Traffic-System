Chapter 1 · Introduction

Overview of the project

Rapid urbanization and rising vehicular density have turned traffic congestion into one of the most pressing infrastructure problems in modern cities. Idling at intersections costs millions of hours in lost productivity, burns excess fuel, and degrades urban air quality.

Most of that congestion traces back to one device: the fixed-time signal controller. These systems allocate a strict, unchanging green-time duration to each lane regardless of how many vehicles are actually waiting — a rigidity that produces what traffic engineers call green-time wastage, where a lane sits empty under a green light while a queue builds on red.

This project replaces the timer with mathematics. By pairing real-time vehicle detection with a deterministic decision engine, the intersection can evaluate its own congestion frame-by-frame and react accordingly — fundamentally redefining how a single intersection manages mobility.

Problem statement

Two operational flaws motivate this work.

1. Green-time wastage and algorithmic rigidity

Fixed-cycle controllers cannot detect real-time queue length. They allocate equal green time to empty lanes and congested lanes alike. Even where physical sensors exist, inductive loop detectors are expensive to install, prone to failure under road wear, and act only as binary presence triggers — not traffic analyzers.

2. The emergency vehicle bottleneck

Legacy infrastructure cannot classify vehicle types, so ambulances queue identically to commuter traffic. This directly threatens the medical "Golden Hour" — the window after trauma where rapid intervention sharply increases survival odds — and forces emergency drivers into the secondary collision risk of running red lights to compensate.

Core need

A software-driven, hardware-agnostic solution that calculates dynamic traffic pressure to eliminate wastage, while providing an absolute, automated preemption protocol for emergency responders.

Project objectives

  • Design a dynamic control algorithm — a Pressure-Based Adaptive Control System (PB-ACS) that replaces static timers with a deterministic, mathematical decision engine.
  • Eliminate green-time wastage — a phase-switching mechanism that detects empty lanes instantly and reallocates priority to congested ones.
  • Implement Priority 0 preemption — a fail-safe interrupt that simulates computer-vision classification (e.g. YOLOv8 detecting an ambulance) and clears the intersection without manual input.
  • Guarantee algorithmic fairness — an anti-starvation metric that factors accumulated wait time so low-volume lanes are never permanently blocked.
  • Construct a digital twin — a hardware-agnostic Python/HTML5 Canvas/PyScript test environment that replicates vehicle kinematics and YOLO-style data structures under stochastic traffic.

Scope & limitations

The study is scoped to the algorithmic and data-processing layers of a single 4-way intersection, validated entirely through a custom 2D kinematic digital twin rather than physical deployment.

LimitationDetail
Single-node architectureNo multi-intersection "Green Wave" synchronization yet.

Advantages & disadvantages

Advantages

  • Dynamic optimization — continuously recalculated pressure eradicates green-time wastage.
  • Priority 0 preemption — instant, safe right-of-way for emergency vehicles.
  • Deterministic reliability — a stateless equation, not a black-box model, so every decision is predictable and auditable.
  • Cost-effective scalability — overhead cameras are cheaper to install and maintain than buried loop detectors.

Disadvantages & challenges

  • Environmental data sensitivity — rain, fog, glare, and occlusion can degrade computer-vision accuracy.
  • Computational overhead — requires dedicated edge hardware at every intersection.
  • Single-node bottlenecking — optimizing one intersection may just push congestion downstream.
  • VRU complexity — pedestrians and cyclists need dedicated phase logic not yet modeled.
Chapter 2 · Literature Review

Evolution of traffic control: fixed vs. actuated

The baseline: fixed-time control

For most of the 20th century, intersections ran on synchronous-motor timing dials executing pre-programmed phase sequences from historical traffic studies — blind to the actual condition of the road.

Webster's delay model (1958)

Timing plans derive from F.V. Webster's queuing-theory framework, which shows that intersection delay forms a parabolic curve against cycle length: too short, and lost clearance time dominates; too long, and vehicles wait needlessly on red.

d = c(1−λ)² / [2(1−λx)] + x² / [2q(1−x)] − 0.65 (c/q²)^⅓ x^(2+5λ)
  • c — cycle length (seconds)
  • λ — proportion of the cycle effectively green (g/c)
  • q — traffic flow (vehicles/sec)
  • x — degree of saturation

Despite the math, fixed-time systems are algorithmically blind to accidents or surges, suffer green-time wastage by design, and degrade over years as urban demographics outpace the original timing study.

Actuated control

Actuated systems add physical sensors and fluctuate cycle length around real demand.

  • Semi-actuated — sensors only on the minor street; the major arterial defaults to permanent green.
  • Fully actuated — sensors on all approaches, governed by Minimum Green, Passage Time, Gap Out, and Max Out timers.
Hard limit

Loop detectors return binary presence only — they cannot tell a 2-car queue from a 50-car queue, and cannot classify vehicle type, ruling out automated emergency preemption.

Inductive loop detectors

An ILD is an electromagnetic LC circuit: a buried copper coil driven at 10–200 kHz. A vehicle's metal mass induces eddy currents that oppose the field (Lenz's Law), dropping the loop's inductance and shifting its resonant frequency.

f = 1 / (2π√LC)

A frequency shift past threshold registers a binary "call." Installation requires sawing a 6×6 ft, 2-inch groove into pavement — making the sensor's life entirely dependent on road integrity. Freeze-thaw cycles snap the copper; resurfacing destroys it outright. When a loop fails, controllers fail-safe into Maximum Recall, assuming a car is always present and reverting to a static timer.

Why ILDs are a dead end for AI routing

  • Binary blind spot — no queue-length awareness, so true pressure cannot be calculated.
  • VRU blindness — poor at detecting bicycles or pedestrians.
  • No classification — an ambulance is electromagnetically indistinguishable from a delivery van.

The paradigm shift: computer vision

Optical detection replaces buried copper with overhead HD cameras mapped to digital Regions of Interest (ROIs). Early background-subtraction approaches were unreliable — shadows and headlight glare triggered false positives — which pushed the field toward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) that genuinely classify what they see.

From R-CNN to YOLO

Two-stage detectors like R-CNN ran under 5 FPS — too slow for vehicles moving at 60 km/h. YOLO (You Only Look Once) reframed detection as a single regression pass, solving the speed problem.

YOLOv8 mechanics

  • Grid division — the frame splits into an S×S grid; each cell detects objects centered within it.
  • Bounding box prediction — each cell predicts x, y, w, h plus a confidence score.
  • Confidence — Pr(Object) × IoU
  • Non-Maximum Suppression — removes duplicate boxes on the same vehicle.

The output — a structured array of coordinates, speed, and class — is exactly what enables Priority 0 preemption: the moment a bounding box is classified as an ambulance, the data bus flags a boolean override straight to the AI Brain.

Reinforcement learning vs. deterministic models

Once vision solves data acquisition, the system still needs a decision-making "brain." Academic literature splits into two camps.

Reinforcement learning

An RL agent learns a policy via the Bellman equation, balancing exploitation against exploration to maximize cumulative reward.

Q(s,a) = Q(s,a) + α[ R + γ·max Q(s′,a′) − Q(s,a) ]

This project explicitly rejects RL for three reasons:

  1. Exploration risk — a random exploratory action at a live intersection could allocate green to conflicting streams.
  2. Curse of dimensionality — weather, VRUs, and emergencies blow up the state space beyond practical convergence.
  3. Black-box liability — a neural net's millions of weights can't be audited after a catastrophic error.

The deterministic alternative

PB-ACS instead derives from Backpressure Routing (Tassiulas & Ephremides, 1992) — originally built for congested wireless packet networks, here adapted so the intersection becomes the router and its four lanes become competing queues. Given identical inputs, the algorithm produces the identical, auditable output every time.

Chapter 3 · System Architecture & Methodology

The digital twin

Mission-critical infrastructure AI faces a paradox: it must be proven safe under real conditions before deployment, yet cannot be deployed until proven safe. The Digital Twin — a concept with roots in NASA's Apollo program — resolves this by simulating real-time, stochastic conditions and applying physical laws to them, without risking a single human life.

Spatial abstraction

The intersection is mapped onto an 800×800 pixel Cartesian matrix. Road width is 240 units across four 60-unit lanes; stop lines are defined relative to the center coordinate.

Kinematic modeling

Three physical systems govern vehicle behavior:

Stochastic spawning

A 60 FPS probability loop spawns vehicles (P(spawn) < 0.08), with a separate 0.5% chance of generating an AMBULANCE class to trigger Priority 0 stress tests.

Braking physics

d = v² / 2a

If the gap to an obstacle falls below this threshold, the engine applies deceleration — producing a realistic stop rather than artificial clipping.

Turning dynamics — cubic Bézier curves

B(t) = (1−t)³P₀ + 3(1−t)²tP₁ + 3(1−t)t²P₂ + t³P₃

Four control points define a smooth, sweeping turn rather than a sharp 90° pivot, keeping collision detection accurate through complex clearing phases.

The point of all this physics

It exists to feed the virtual YOLOv8 emulator. Every frame, collect_sensor_data() checks each vehicle against the ROIs and extracts volume, wait time, and classification — structurally identical to a real camera's JSON payload. The AI Brain never knows it's in a simulation.

System architecture pipeline

The pipeline runs as four decoupled, asynchronous phases — designed so the AI Brain can be lifted out and dropped onto real edge hardware without rewriting its logic.

  1. Phase 1 — Data extraction (Environment Engine)Virtual sensor polling at 60 FPS checks every vehicle's coordinates against a 300-pixel ROI per lane, extracting presence, delay, and classification.
  2. Phase 2 — Payload formatting (Data Bus)Raw metrics serialize into sensor_data and emergency_stats dictionaries — the structural equivalent of a JSON payload over Ethernet.
  3. Phase 3 — Decision logic (AI Brain)TrafficBrain.get_decision() checks emergencies first, then runs the PB-ACS equations across all four nodes and returns the optimal phase string.
  4. Phase 4 — Actuation & telemetryThe returned state drives the rendering layer while the Analytics module silently logs throughput, standard delay, and Priority 0 delay.

PB-ACS methodology

Volume alone can't define congestion — a major artery would hold green forever while a single car on a side street starves indefinitely. PB-ACS instead scores each lane on a multivariate equation combining volume and accumulated delay.

Pi = (α × Vi) + (β × Σ Wi,k)
  • V_i — vehicle count queued in lane i
  • ΣW_i,k — sum of accumulated wait times across all vehicles in lane i
  • α — volume weight hyperparameter
  • β — wait-time (anti-starvation) weight

Worked starvation example

Lane A: 20 freshly arrived vehicles. Lane B: 1 vehicle waiting 120 seconds. With α=1, β=0, lane B is invisible (score 1 vs. 20). Tuning to α=1, β=0.5 changes everything:

The accumulated delay flips the outcome — the AI grants right-of-way to the starved minor lane without needing an arbitrary maximum-red timer.

Finite state machine & hysteresis

  1. Pressure calculationCompute P for North, South, East, West from the live payload.
  2. State evaluationTarget phase = the direction with max(P_i).
  3. Minimum green checkIf the active phase is under its minimum threshold (e.g. 5s), reject the switch — preventing flicker.
  4. Clearance (orange)Downgrade to ORANGE for a fixed clearance interval before switching, giving vehicles time to safely exit.
  5. State commitOnce clearance expires, commit to the new green phase. Wait times reset and the optimization loop restarts.

Priority 0: emergency preemption

Mathematical fairness is irrelevant to an ambulance. Legacy hardware solutions — 3M Opticom infrared strobes, acoustic siren sensors — suffer high cost, line-of-sight failure behind large vehicles, and urban acoustic noise that confuses directionality. PB-ACS achieves preemption entirely through the existing vision pipeline.

If Σ Ei > 0 for i ∈ {N,S,E,W} → execute Priority 0

Once any emergency flag fires, the brain abandons pressure math entirely and runs a non-interruptible clearance sequence:

  1. Forced orangeConflicting lanes transition to orange, giving commuters standard braking distance.
  2. All-red clearanceA 2.0-second universal red ensures the intersection box is physically empty.
  3. Priority green allocationExclusive green granted to the emergency vehicle's lane.
  4. Hold stateGreen holds — ignoring rising cross-street pressure — until the vehicle's bounding box exits the far side.

Post-preemption recovery

Cross-streets accumulate extreme wait-time values during the override. Because P_i includes ΣW_i,k, those starved lanes automatically post the highest pressure scores the instant normal operation resumes — the system self-heals without any special-case recovery code.

Chapter 4 · Implementation Details

Environment & physics engine

The physics proxy lives in simulation.py, instantiating every vehicle as an OOP Vehicle object with spatial (x, y, direction), kinematic (speed, max_speed, acceleration), telemetry (waiting_time), and classification (vehicle_type) attributes.

Braking distance derivation

v² = u² + 2as → dbrake = u² / 2|a| + dsafe

If the gap to an obstacle drops below d_brake, the engine overwrites the acceleration vector with a deceleration value, smoothly interpolating speed to zero.

Bézier turning

Four control points — origin, two curvature controls, destination — drive the parametric equation as t sweeps 0→1, keeping turning vehicles spatially accurate for collision detection.

Virtual sensors & ROI extraction

Each ROI is an Axis-Aligned Bounding Box defined by four thresholds. For the northbound approach, relative to intersection center (Cx, Cy):

  • x_min = Cx − Lane_Width
  • x_max = Cx
  • y_min = Cy − Intersection_Radius − SENSOR_DEPTH
  • y_max = Cy − Intersection_Radius

A Point-in-Rectangle test runs at 60 FPS against every active vehicle:

is_in_ROI = (Vx ≥ xmin) ∧ (Vx ≤ xmax) ∧ (Vy ≥ ymin) ∧ (Vy ≤ ymax)

A detected vehicle contributes a volume increment, a wait-time accumulation, and — if its type matches AMBULANCE — an emergency boolean flip, all packaged into a payload structurally identical to a real edge-camera's output:

sensor_payload = {
    'N': {'count': 14, 'total_wait': 1250, 'emergency': False},
    'S': {'count': 2,  'total_wait': 45,   'emergency': False},
    'E': {'count': 8,  'total_wait': 400,  'emergency': True},
    'W': {'count': 0,  'total_wait': 0,    'emergency': False}
}

This strict decoupling means simulation.py can be deleted entirely and replaced with a live YOLOv8 camera script — the AI Brain never needs a single line rewritten.

AI decision logic (traffic_ai.py)

The brain is a stateless finite state machine — no hidden memory, no learned weights, just a rigid hierarchy of logic gates evaluated fresh on every call.

Top-level gate: Priority 0

if any(lane['emergency'] == True for lane in sensor_payload.values()):
    return execute_emergency_preemption(sensor_payload)

PB-ACS computational engine

ALPHA = 1.0  # volume weight
BETA  = 0.5  # accumulated-delay weight

pressure_scores = {}
for direction, data in sensor_payload.items():
    volume = data['count']
    delay  = data['total_wait']
    pressure = (ALPHA * volume) + (BETA * delay)
    pressure_scores[direction] = pressure

target_phase = max(pressure_scores, key=pressure_scores.get)

Hysteresis & clearance generation

If the active phase hasn't reached MIN_GREEN_TIME (5.0s), the requested switch is rejected outright to prevent thrashing. Otherwise the brain string-manipulates the current state into its clearance form — N_GREEN → N_ORANGE — and holds it for a fixed 3.0-second timer before finally committing the new phase.

Real-time analytics (analytics.py)

Built on the Observer pattern — analytics watches the simulation passively and never influences it. A "despawn lifecycle hook" captures each vehicle's final telemetry the instant before it's removed from memory.

KPI formulas

  • Average standard delay — mean of accumulated_wait_time across all standard vehicles cleared
  • Average Priority 0 delay — isolated mean wait time for AMBULANCE-class vehicles only
  • Throughput (Q) — N_total / T_sim, vehicles cleared per minute

Results are pushed live to the DOM via PyScript — Python reaching directly into document.getElementById() to update dashboard text — and the full dataset serializes to CSV for post-run analysis in Pandas and Matplotlib.

Algorithmic limitations & edge cases

Downstream spillback

The base equation assumes infinite downstream capacity. A future version needs a capacity-penalty term:

Pi = (α × Vi) + (β × ΣWi,k) − (γ × Cdown)

Sensor occlusion

A large truck can hide an entire queue of cars behind it — the system would see V=1 instead of 16, miscalculating pressure and unfairly starving the occluded vehicles. Weather (rain, fog, glare) compounds this; loop detectors, by contrast, are weather-immune.

Hyperparameter brittleness

α and β are hard-coded for one traffic pattern. As real-world conditions shift (concept drift), a stateless deterministic system cannot self-adjust — it requires manual re-tuning.

Conflicting Priority 0 invocations

If two emergency vehicles approach from perpendicular streets simultaneously, the boolean array creates a logic deadlock with no built-in tiebreaker. The proposed fix is a Time-to-Collision arbitration:

TTC = Distancestopline / Velocitycurrent

— granting Priority 0 to whichever vehicle has the lower TTC, holding the second safely until the first clears.

Four findings stand out from the identical-timeline simulations:

  • Architectural viability — a decoupled Python/Canvas/PyScript pipeline can fully emulate a YOLOv8 vision pipeline; the AI Brain is genuinely hardware-agnostic and portable to edge devices without rewrite.
  • Wastage eradicated — 842.0 seconds of green-time wastage in the baseline dropped to zero under PB-ACS.
  • Delay compressed, not just reduced — the standard deviation collapse (28.5s → 8.2s) shows the anti-starvation term enforces equity, not just average speed-up.
  • Emergency response transformed — a 57.1% stop probability fell to 0.0%, with full self-healing recovery within roughly 115 seconds post-preemption.

Future scope

Edge computing integration

Move the AI Brain onto physical edge hardware (e.g. NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano) inside the roadside cabinet, so only lightweight JSON telemetry — never raw video — needs to travel anywhere.

Heterogeneous, non-lane-based traffic

Adapt the pressure equation with per-class Passenger Car Equivalent weights for mixed traffic conditions (two-wheelers, auto-rickshaws, buses) common outside strict lane-discipline road systems:

Pi = Σc∈C (αc × Vi,c) + (β × ΣWi,k)

Federated learning across the grid

Individual intersections could share lightweight gradient updates — not raw video — to a central aggregator using Federated Averaging, letting the grid learn city-wide rhythms and pre-synchronize green waves before platoons even arrive.

wt+1 = Σk=1K (nk/n) wkt+1

Economic case

Lower total cost of ownership than legacy hardware, plus the macroeconomic upside of a 56.8% idling-delay cut: less wasted fuel, lower localized emissions, and measurable logistics efficiency gains for a city procurement case.

The future of urban mobility does not lie in pouring more concrete or enforcing rigid rules on dynamic populations — it lies in data.
